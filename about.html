Current Research

I am a Postdoctoral Researcher at the University of Utah, where I study the thermodynamics, kinetics, and molecular mechanisms of ice formation and water’s phase behavior. My recent work includes simulations of ice nucleation at topographical active sites, non-equilibrium relaxation in deeply supercooled water, and amorphous ice transformations. These studies are relevant to atmospheric science, cryopreservation, materials science, and the physics of metastable liquids. I've contributed to high-impact publications in journals such as Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, Journal of the American Chemical Society, The Journal of Physical Chemistry, and Atmospheric Chemistry and Physics.

More recently, I have been working on 2D molecular crystals relevant to pharmaceutical manufacturing. I use molecular simulations to understand how crystal polymorphs transform under mechanical deformation, such as the stresses present during ball milling. The goal is to identify how microscopic structural features control macroscopic mechanical behavior, polymorphic transformations, and amorphization pathways.
